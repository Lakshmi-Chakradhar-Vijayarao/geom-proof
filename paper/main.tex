% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Can we predict the performance of a hallucination detector without training one?
We investigate whether Fisher separability~$J$ provides a closed-form,
geometry-based certificate for detecting surface-form hallucinations in large
language models.  We show that the Gaussian approximation
$\Phi\!\left(\sqrt{J}/2\right)$ closely matches linear probe AUROC, with
$\leq\!2.7\%$ maximum error and $0.93\%$ mean error across five model sizes and
two architectures (Qwen~2.5 and GPT-2).  However, the fundamental task
definition is limited: ROUGE-L and GPT-4o labels disagree at Cohen's
$\kappa = -0.010$, indicating that this pipeline detects \emph{surface-form
divergence} rather than factual incorrectness.  We further show that
hidden-state distributions are empirically non-Gaussian: Sliced Wasserstein
distance ($\text{SW}_2$) correlates more strongly with AUROC on
Qwen~2.5~3B ($\rho = 0.821$ vs.\ $0.458$ for Fisher), but this advantage
reverses on GPT-2~Medium ($\rho = -0.237$ vs.\ $0.395$), exposing a
model-dependent geometry.  Split conformal prediction yields formal in-distribution
guarantees ($\alpha^* = 0.07$ at $52.9\%$ acceptance for Qwen), which collapse
under distribution shift (AUROC~$\to 0.485$).  Taken together, these results
establish Fisher separability as an empirically reliable---and precisely
scoped---geometric certificate: it bounds probe performance under documented
assumptions while its failure modes characterize the boundaries of
geometry-based hallucination governance.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Large language models (LLMs) generate fluent text that is nonetheless
factually incorrect---a phenomenon termed hallucination.  Detecting hallucinations
before output reaches a user is a prerequisite for safe deployment, yet most
detection approaches require labelled training data, a trained probe, or multiple
model samples~\citep{manakul2023selfcheckgpt,chen2024inside}.

An appealing alternative is a \emph{geometry-based certificate}: a quantity
computable from hidden-state statistics alone that predicts, before any probe
is trained, whether a linear detector can achieve a given AUROC.  If such a
certificate exists, it becomes possible to assess model reliability at deployment
time without ground-truth labels or supervised training.

Fisher separability $J$ is a natural candidate.  Under Gaussian equal-covariance
assumptions, the maximum AUROC achievable by any linear classifier on two class
distributions is $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$, where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.
$J$ is computable from the first two moments of the hidden-state distribution
at each layer, requiring no labels beyond the class centroids.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item We derive the Gaussian certificate $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ from first
    principles and validate it empirically: bound error $\leq 2.7\%$ across all
    layers and both tested architectures (Exp.~01, 06).
  \item We show via 5-fold cross-validation that the certificate predicts
    unseen-fold AUROC within $0.93\%$ mean error before any probe is trained
    (Exp.~03).
  \item We identify a fundamental limitation: Fisher $J$ cannot select the
    optimal probing layer (0\% argmax match rate).  The certificate bounds
    achievable performance but not where to find it (Exp.~03).
  \item We characterize the non-Gaussian geometry: $J \approx W_2^2$ only
    approximately (mean relative error $1.71$ for Qwen), and $\text{SW}_2$
    outperforms Fisher only on Qwen---not on GPT-2 (Exp.~08).
  \item We derive and validate a split conformal guarantee
    ($\alpha^* = 0.07$, Qwen; $\alpha^* = 0.06$, GPT-2) and show it collapses
    under out-of-distribution shift (Exp.~10).
  \item We document that the task itself is ambiguous: ROUGE-L and GPT-4o labels
    disagree at $\kappa = -0.010$ (Exp.~07), scoping all results to
    surface-form divergence.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Fisher Discriminant and Linear Separability}
\label{sec:background-fisher}

Let $\mathcal{H}_c, \mathcal{H}_h \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the sets of
hidden states for correct and hallucinated responses at a given layer $L$.
Define class centroids $\mu_c, \mu_h \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and the pooled
within-class covariance
$\Sigma_w = \frac{n_c \Sigma_c + n_h \Sigma_h}{n_c + n_h}.$
The Fisher ratio is
\begin{equation}
  J(L) = (\mu_c - \mu_h)^\top \Sigma_w^{-1} (\mu_c - \mu_h).
  \label{eq:fisher}
\end{equation}
Under the Gaussian equal-covariance model
$P(h \mid y) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_y, \Sigma_w)$,
the Bayes-optimal linear classifier on the Mahalanobis-projected line
achieves
\begin{equation}
  \text{AUROC} = \Phi\!\left(\frac{\sqrt{J}}{2}\right),
  \label{eq:auroc-bound}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.  This follows from the fact that the
linear score $s(h) = (\mu_c - \mu_h)^\top \Sigma_w^{-1} h$ is normally
distributed under each class with means separated by $\sqrt{J}$ and unit
variance---the false-positive rate at threshold $t$ is
$\Phi(t - \sqrt{J}/2)$ and the true-positive rate is $\Phi(t + \sqrt{J}/2)$,
giving $\text{AUROC} = \Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ by integrating over $t$.

We use this as an \textit{approximation} rather than a strict bound: real
distributions are non-Gaussian, so \eqref{eq:auroc-bound} may be loose.

\subsection{Optimal Transport and the Gaussian Limit}
\label{sec:background-ot}

The $2$-Wasserstein distance between distributions $P, Q$ is
$W_2(P, Q) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(P,Q)} \bigl(\mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim\gamma}
\|x-y\|^2\bigr)^{1/2}.$
When both distributions are Gaussian with equal covariance $\Sigma_w$,
the whitened distance satisfies $W_2^2 = J$, making Fisher ratio the
\emph{Gaussian special case} of optimal transport~\citep{sugiyama2007}.
Sliced Wasserstein distance ($\text{SW}_2$) approximates $W_2$ via random
one-dimensional projections and makes no Gaussianity assumption, providing
a distribution-free alternative certificate~\citep{kolouri2019}.

\subsection{Conformal Prediction}
\label{sec:background-conformal}

Split conformal prediction~\citep{angelopoulos2022} provides finite-sample
coverage guarantees under exchangeability.  Given a calibration set
$\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, a threshold $\tau$ is chosen such that the
prediction set $\mathcal{C}(x) = \{y : s(x,y) \leq \tau\}$ satisfies
$P(y \in \mathcal{C}(x)) \geq 1 - \alpha$.  In our setting, $s(x,y)$ is
the probe's hallucination probability score; ACCEPT corresponds to
$s(x) \leq \tau$; and the guarantee becomes
$P(\text{hallucinated} \mid \text{accepted}) \leq \alpha$.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Task and Datasets}
\label{sec:setup-task}

We study binary classification of LLM responses as \emph{correct} or
\emph{hallucinated}.  All experiments use two datasets:

\noindent\textbf{HaluEval}~\citep{li2023halueval}: 2{,}000 question–answer
pairs (in-distribution).  Model responses are generated and labelled against
reference answers.

\noindent\textbf{TruthfulQA}~\citep{lin2022truthfulqa}: 400 questions used
exclusively as an out-of-distribution (OOD) test set.

\subsection{A Note on Label Validity}
\label{sec:setup-labels}

Labels are derived from ROUGE-L matching (threshold $\geq 0.4$): a response
scoring below the threshold is labelled hallucinated.  To audit this choice,
we re-labelled 700 HaluEval responses using GPT-4o as a judge (Exp.~07).
The agreement between ROUGE-L and GPT-4o labels is Cohen's $\kappa = -0.010$
(ROUGE-L hallucination rate $= 0.993$; GPT-4o rate $= 0.981$).  Near-zero
$\kappa$ indicates the two labelling strategies identify \emph{different}
samples as hallucinated, despite similar rates.

\textbf{Scope condition}: \emph{All results in this paper describe
surface-form divergence (ROUGE-L failure), not factual incorrectness.}
These are correlated but empirically distinct.

\subsection{Hidden-State Extraction}
\label{sec:setup-extraction}

For each input, we extract the last-token hidden state $h_L \in \mathbb{R}^d$

throughout, consistent with prior work~\citep{azaria2023internal,

\subsection{Fisher Separability Computation}
\label{sec:setup-fisher}

Direct inversion of $\Sigma_w \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is numerically
unstable at $d = 2048$.  We apply two complementary approximations:
(i) \textbf{PCA projection} to $k = 100$ principal components
(capturing $> 95\%$ of variance in practice); and
(ii) \textbf{Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage}~\citep{ledoit2004} for covariance
regularization.  Both methods produce consistent $J$ estimates;
PCA projection is used throughout.  We note that for the Qwen 0.5B and
1.5B models at $n = 400$, the aspect ratio $\gamma = d/n > 1$
(values 2.24 and 3.84 respectively), making full-rank covariance estimation
unreliable; these results should be interpreted accordingly.

\section{The Geometric Certificate}
\label{sec:certificate}

\subsection{Derivation}
\label{sec:cert-derivation}

\begin{proposition}[Gaussian AUROC Certificate]
\label{prop:cert}
Let $P_c = \mathcal{N}(\mu_c, \Sigma_w)$ and
$P_h = \mathcal{N}(\mu_h, \Sigma_w)$ with equal covariance $\Sigma_w$.
The AUROC of the Bayes-optimal linear classifier equals
$\Phi\!\left(\nicefrac{\sqrt{J}}{2}\right)$
where $J = (\mu_c - \mu_h)^\top \Sigma_w^{-1} (\mu_c - \mu_h)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The optimal score is $s(h) = w^\top h$ with
$w = \Sigma_w^{-1}(\mu_c - \mu_h)$.
Under $P_c$: $s \sim \mathcal{N}(J,\, J)$;
under $P_h$: $s \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, J)$.
The standardised gap is $\sqrt{J}$, and
$\text{AUROC} = P(s_c > s_h) = \Phi\!\left(\nicefrac{\sqrt{J}}{2}\right)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Proposition~\ref{prop:cert} assumes Gaussian equal-covariance distributions.
Real hidden states violate both assumptions; Eq.~\eqref{eq:auroc-bound}
therefore serves as an approximation whose tightness we measure empirically.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Layer Selection Problem}
\label{sec:cert-layer}

Fisher $J$ cannot identify the optimal probing layer.  In all five CV folds,
argmax$_L\, J(L)$ selects L25--L26, while oracle best probe layers range from
L10 to L36 across folds.  A depth-weighted heuristic
($J(L) \times L / L_{\max}$) achieves 20\% match rate---better than
argmax but still poor.

\begin{remark}
The failure arises because $J$ measures second-order geometry (class
separation by covariance-weighted distance), whereas probe AUROC reflects
the total discriminative structure including local density variations and
higher-order moments.  High $J$ implies high \emph{achievable} AUROC; it
does not identify which layer's probe will realise that potential.
\end{remark}

The practical implication: the certificate is useful for deciding \emph{whether}
to probe, not \emph{where}.

\section{Beyond Gaussianity: Wasserstein Certificates}
\label{sec:ot}

\subsection{The Fisher--Wasserstein Identity and Its Violation}
\label{sec:ot-identity}

Under Gaussian equal-covariance assumptions, Fisher separability and
Wasserstein-2 distance in whitened space are identical:
$J = W_2^2\!\left(\tilde{P}_c, \tilde{P}_h\right)$
where $\tilde{P} = \Sigma_w^{-1/2} P$.
We verify this identity at five representative layers per model (Exp.~08).
Table~\ref{tab:identity} reports relative errors
$\epsilon = |J - W_{2,\text{wh}}^2| / J$.

\begin{remark}
A plausible mechanistic explanation: in Qwen~2.5~3B, later layers compress
representations toward well-separated class manifolds, making distributional
divergence (SW$_2$) a better proxy for linear separability.  In GPT-2~Medium,
the probe peak at $33\%$ depth (L8) precedes the representational compression
phase, so distributional divergence and probe performance evolve in opposite
directions.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Distribution Shift: Guarantee Collapse}
\label{sec:conformal-ood}

We test the calibrated threshold from HaluEval on 400 TruthfulQA
questions---a genuinely different task distribution.  Results:
AUROC $= 0.485$ (chance level); $98.2\%$ of TruthfulQA responses are
hallucinated under ROUGE-L labels; acceptance rate $= 99.95\%$ (nearly
all accepted).  The conformal guarantee, calibrated at $\alpha^* = 0.07$
on HaluEval, would report an empirical hallucination rate of $0.982$
on TruthfulQA---a $14\times$ violation.

\begin{remark}
The guarantee holds only under exchangeability (i.i.d.\ with the
calibration distribution).  OOD shift violates this assumption.
Distribution-robust conformal prediction~\citep{tibshirani2019conformal}
is the principled extension, but requires density ratio estimation
between calibration and deployment distributions.
\end{remark}

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]

\item \textbf{Label quality.}  Cohen's $\kappa = -0.010$ between ROUGE-L
  and GPT-4o labels (Exp.~07) means every AUROC and every bound in this
  paper conditions on a labelling criterion that a semantic judge largely
  disagrees with.  Future work should establish a validated labelling
  protocol---ideally with inter-annotator agreement on factual
  incorrectness---before drawing strong empirical conclusions.

\item \textbf{Gaussian violations and bound looseness.}  The Gaussian
  equal-covariance assumption is violated ($J \neq W_2^2$, mean relative
  error up to $1.71$).  Bound errors of $1$--$3\%$ result.  Fisher $J$
  uniformly over-predicts AUROC, but only by amounts small enough to be
  practically useful.

\item \textbf{Layer selection.}  Argmax $J$ never identifies the best
  probe layer (0\% match over 5 CV folds).  The certificate bounds
  achievable performance; it does not locate the layer that achieves it.

\item \textbf{Scale curve is interpolation.}  $R^2 = 0.9996$ on three
  data points with two parameters is trivially high.  We interpret the
  result as demonstrating monotonic growth, not as a scaling law.

\item \textbf{Aspect ratio violation at small scale.}  Qwen 0.5B
  ($\gamma = 2.24$) and 1.5B ($\gamma = 3.84$) have $d > n$ at $n = 400$.
  Covariance estimation is unreliable in this regime; Ledoit--Wolf
  shrinkage mitigates but does not resolve this.

\item \textbf{Architecture transfer unresolved.}  We attempted transfer
  to Mamba-130m (SSM, 25 layers) but the experiment was confounded by the
  absence of instruction tuning: all responses were labelled hallucinated
  (hall rate $= 1.0$), making classification meaningless.  SSM
  architecture transfer is an open question.

\item \textbf{Conformal guarantee is distribution-conditional.}  The
  $\alpha^* = 0.07$ guarantee holds only on HaluEval.  OOD applications
  require covariate-shift conformal methods.

\item \textbf{SW$_2$ advantage is model-dependent.}  SW$_2$ outperforms
  Fisher on Qwen but underperforms on GPT-2.  No universally superior
  distribution-free certificate exists across the models tested.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Representation probing for hallucination.}
\citet{burns2022discovering} show that LLM hidden states encode linearly
separable truthfulness signals.  \citet{azaria2023internal} (SAPLMA) apply
this to GPT-2, establishing a global linear probe baseline.
\citet{chen2024inside} (INSIDE) use global covariance eigenvalues;
\citet{kossen2024semantic} train probes to predict semantic entropy
from single-pass hidden states with OOD robustness.
\citet{du2024haloscope} project embeddings onto an SVD hallucination
subspace.  Our work differs by providing a \emph{training-free} bound
on what any such probe can achieve, rather than training a probe itself.

\paragraph{Geometric and distributional structure.}
\citet{marks2024geometry} show that truth representations exhibit emergent
linear structure across LLM families.  \citet{belrose2023eliciting} track
prediction formation layer-by-layer via the tuned lens.  Neither work
derives a computable certificate from the geometry.

\paragraph{Conformal prediction.}
\citet{angelopoulos2022} provide the foundational framework.
\citet{tibshirani2019conformal} extend conformal prediction to handle
distribution shift via importance weighting---the principled extension
needed for OOD deployment of the certificate derived here.
\citet{abbasi2024conformal} apply conformal abstention to LLM generation,
requiring multiple samples; our method uses a single forward pass.

\paragraph{Output-level detection.}
\citet{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} (SelfCheckGPT) detect hallucination via
multi-sample output consistency without accessing hidden states.
\citet{li2023halueval} provide the HaluEval benchmark.

\paragraph{Local geometry and KNN methods.}
\citet{sun2022ood} use KNN distance (not class separability) for OOD
detection in image models.  \citet{sugiyama2007} establish local Fisher
discriminant analysis at training time.  Our approach uses Fisher
separability in KNN neighbourhoods at inference time (the GUARDIAN system,
described separately), addressing the global/local failure documented
in multi-step pipelines.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that Fisher separability provides a computable, training-free
geometric certificate for linear probe performance in hallucination
detection.  The approximation $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ matches probe AUROC within
$2.7\%$ maximum error across all tested layers and architectures, and
predicts held-out fold AUROC within $0.93\%$ mean error in cross-validation.
A split conformal guarantee ($\alpha^* = 0.07$) provides a formal
in-distribution bound on hallucination rate among accepted queries.

Equally important are the failure modes, each precisely characterised:
the certificate cannot select the optimal probing layer (0\% argmax
match); the Gaussian assumption is violated ($J \neq W_2^2$, mean error
$1.71$ for Qwen); the SW$_2$ advantage over Fisher is model-dependent
(reversed on GPT-2); and the conformal guarantee collapses under
distribution shift (AUROC $\to 0.485$, TruthfulQA).

These failures are not incidental: they constitute a roadmap.
Distribution-free certificates via Wasserstein geometry, local geometry
adaptation for OOD robustness, and validated labelling protocols
beyond ROUGE-L are the three concrete next steps.  The geometry works;
the Gaussian assumption is the limiting factor.
