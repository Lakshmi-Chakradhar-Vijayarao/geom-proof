% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Can the performance of a hallucination detector be predicted from representation
geometry---before any probe is trained?  We study this question by investigating
Fisher separability~$J$ as a closed-form, training-free certificate for linear
probing of surface-form hallucinations in large language models.  The key
distinction from prior geometric approaches is that we do not use $J$ as a
classifier; we use it to \emph{bound} what any linear classifier can achieve.

Empirically, the Gaussian approximation $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ matches linear probe
AUROC within $\leq 2.7\%$ maximum error and $1.2$--$1.4\%$ mean error across
two fully-analysed architectures (Qwen~2.5~3B and GPT-2~Medium); 5-fold
cross-validation on Qwen~2.5~3B alone yields $0.93\%$ mean error before any
label is used for training.  Critically, the bound holds for a shallow MLP probe (64 hidden,
ReLU) as well as the linear probe: zero bound violations across all 62
layer--model combinations, with MLP gain over linear $\leq 0.09\%$, suggesting
that Gaussian slack is sufficient to accommodate the tested degree of
nonlinearity.

Two principled limitations are precisely characterised.
\textit{Label validity}: ROUGE-L and GPT-4o labels disagree at Cohen's
$\kappa = -0.010$, scoping all results to \emph{surface-form divergence}
rather than factual incorrectness.
\textit{Distribution shift}: split conformal guarantees ($\alpha^* = 0.07$,
$52.9\%$ acceptance for Qwen) collapse under OOD transfer
(AUROC~$\to 0.485$, $14\times$ violation).

Further, hidden-state geometry is measurably non-Gaussian: Sliced Wasserstein
($\text{SW}_2$) outperforms Fisher on Qwen~2.5~3B ($\rho = 0.821$
vs.~$0.458$) but reverses on GPT-2~Medium ($\rho = -0.237$ vs.~$0.395$),
exposing architecture-dependent geometry.  Fisher $J$ cannot identify the
optimal probing layer (0\% argmax match across all CV folds).

Taken together, the certificate is empirically reliable and precisely scoped:
it bounds probe performance under documented assumptions, and its failure modes
map the boundaries of geometry-based hallucination governance.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Fluent text and factual correctness are distinct properties of language model
output---a mismatch exploited by the hallucination phenomenon.  Intercepting
hallucinated responses before they reach a user is a prerequisite for safe
deployment, yet prevailing detection methods depend on labelled training data,
a supervised probe, or repeated sampling~\citep{manakul2023selfcheckgpt,chen2024inside}.

A qualitatively different approach is a \emph{geometry-based certificate}: a
scalar computable from hidden-state statistics alone that predicts, before any
probe is trained, whether a linear detector can achieve a target AUROC.
Availability of such a certificate would recast hallucination detection as a
representation-level property assessable at deployment time---without
ground-truth labels or supervised training.

Fisher separability $J$ is the natural candidate.  Under Gaussian
equal-covariance assumptions, the maximum AUROC achievable by any linear
classifier on two class distributions equals $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$, where
$\Phi$ denotes the standard normal CDF.  Critically, $J$ depends only on the
first two moments of the hidden-state distribution at each layer and requires
no labels beyond the class centroids.

\textbf{Certificate vs.\ classifier.}
This framing is categorically distinct from using Fisher geometry as a
decision boundary.  Concurrent work~\citep{ricco2026geometric} applies Fisher
separability as a classifier to detect hallucinations in small-model embedding
space, achieving F1~$>90\%$.  The contribution here is orthogonal: rather than
committing to a specific Fisher-based detector, we ask what AUROC ceiling the
geometry imposes on \emph{any} linear classifier at a given layer---before a
single training example is used.  A certificate is \emph{diagnostic}: it
characterises whether a representation supports detection.
A classifier is \emph{operative}: it exploits that support.
Table~\ref{tab:scope} clarifies what this certificate provides and what it
does not.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item We derive the Gaussian certificate $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ from first
    principles and validate it empirically: bound error $\leq 2.7\%$ maximum,
    $1.2$--$1.4\%$ mean, across all layers for Qwen~2.5~3B and GPT-2~Medium
    (Exp.~01, 06).
  \item We show via 5-fold cross-validation that the certificate predicts
    unseen-fold AUROC within $0.93\%$ mean error on Qwen~2.5~3B before any
    probe is trained (Exp.~03).
  \item We identify a fundamental limitation: Fisher $J$ cannot select the
    optimal probing layer (0\% argmax match rate).  The certificate bounds
    achievable performance but not where to find it (Exp.~03).
  \item We characterize the non-Gaussian geometry: $J \approx W_2^2$ only
    approximately (mean relative error $1.71$ for Qwen), and $\text{SW}_2$
    outperforms Fisher only on Qwen---not on GPT-2 (Exp.~08).
  \item We derive and validate a split conformal guarantee
    ($\alpha^* = 0.07$, Qwen; $\alpha^* = 0.06$, GPT-2) and show it collapses
    under out-of-distribution shift (Exp.~10).
  \item We document that the task itself is ambiguous: ROUGE-L and GPT-4o labels
    disagree at $\kappa = -0.010$ (Exp.~07), scoping all results to
    surface-form divergence.
  \item We show that the bound holds empirically for shallow MLP probes as well
    as linear ones: zero violations across 62 layer--model pairs, MLP gain
    $\leq 0.09\%$ over linear (Exp.~12).
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Central thesis.}
Detection performance is a property of representation geometry, not of the
probe.  Fisher separability is a classical discriminant criterion; what has
not been systematically examined is whether it constitutes a \emph{predictive,
training-free certificate} for linear probe performance specifically in large
language models.  This paper provides that examination---with quantitative
validation across architectures, failure-mode characterisation, and a formal
coverage guarantee.  The classifier is, in this framing, merely a
\emph{measurement instrument} reading a signal already encoded in the geometry.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Fisher Discriminant and Linear Separability}
\label{sec:background-fisher}

Let $\mathcal{H}_c, \mathcal{H}_h \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the sets of
hidden states for correct and hallucinated responses at a given layer $L$.
Define class centroids $\mu_c, \mu_h \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and the pooled
within-class covariance
$\Sigma_w = \frac{n_c \Sigma_c + n_h \Sigma_h}{n_c + n_h}.$
The Fisher ratio is
\begin{equation}
  J(L) = (\mu_c - \mu_h)^\top \Sigma_w^{-1} (\mu_c - \mu_h).
  \label{eq:fisher}
\end{equation}
Under the Gaussian equal-covariance model
$P(h \mid y) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_y, \Sigma_w)$,
the Bayes-optimal linear classifier on the Mahalanobis-projected line
achieves
\begin{equation}
  \text{AUROC} = \Phi\!\left(\frac{\sqrt{J}}{2}\right),
  \label{eq:auroc-bound}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.  This follows from the fact that the
linear score $s(h) = (\mu_c - \mu_h)^\top \Sigma_w^{-1} h$ is normally
distributed under each class with means separated by $\sqrt{J}$ and unit
variance---the false-positive rate at threshold $t$ is
$\Phi(t - \sqrt{J}/2)$ and the true-positive rate is $\Phi(t + \sqrt{J}/2)$,
giving $\text{AUROC} = \Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ by integrating over $t$.

We use this as an \textit{approximation} rather than a strict bound: real
distributions are non-Gaussian, so \eqref{eq:auroc-bound} may be loose.

\subsection{Optimal Transport and the Gaussian Limit}
\label{sec:background-ot}

The $2$-Wasserstein distance between distributions $P, Q$ is
$W_2(P, Q) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(P,Q)} \bigl(\mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim\gamma}
\|x-y\|^2\bigr)^{1/2}.$
When both distributions are Gaussian with equal covariance $\Sigma_w$,
the whitened distance satisfies $W_2^2 = J$, making Fisher ratio the
\emph{Gaussian special case} of optimal transport~\citep{sugiyama2007}.
Sliced Wasserstein distance ($\text{SW}_2$) approximates $W_2$ via random
one-dimensional projections and makes no Gaussianity assumption, providing
a distribution-free alternative certificate~\citep{kolouri2019}.

\subsection{Conformal Prediction}
\label{sec:background-conformal}

Split conformal prediction~\citep{angelopoulos2022} provides finite-sample
coverage guarantees under exchangeability.  Given a calibration set
$\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, a threshold $\tau$ is chosen such that the
prediction set $\mathcal{C}(x) = \{y : s(x,y) \leq \tau\}$ satisfies
$P(y \in \mathcal{C}(x)) \geq 1 - \alpha$.  In our setting, $s(x,y)$ is
the probe's hallucination probability score; ACCEPT corresponds to
$s(x) \leq \tau$; and the guarantee becomes
$P(\text{hallucinated} \mid \text{accepted}) \leq \alpha$.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Task and Datasets}
\label{sec:setup-task}

We study binary classification of LLM responses as \emph{correct} or
\emph{hallucinated}.  All experiments use two datasets:

\noindent\textbf{HaluEval}~\citep{li2023halueval}: 2{,}000 question--answer
pairs (in-distribution).  Model responses are generated and labelled against
reference answers.

\noindent\textbf{TruthfulQA}~\citep{lin2022truthfulqa}: 400 questions used
exclusively as an out-of-distribution (OOD) test set.

\subsection{A Note on Label Validity}
\label{sec:setup-labels}

Labels are derived from ROUGE-L matching (threshold $\geq 0.4$): a response
scoring below the threshold is labelled hallucinated.  To audit this choice,
we re-labelled 700 HaluEval responses using GPT-4o as a judge (Exp.~07).
The agreement between ROUGE-L and GPT-4o labels is Cohen's $\kappa = -0.010$
(ROUGE-L hallucination rate $= 0.993$; GPT-4o rate $= 0.981$).  Near-zero
$\kappa$ indicates the two labelling strategies identify \emph{different}
samples as hallucinated, despite similar rates.

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Scope condition (applies throughout):}
\textit{All AUROC values, certificate bounds, and conformal guarantees in
this paper condition on ROUGE-L labels.  They measure surface-form divergence
from reference text, not factual incorrectness.  The $\kappa = -0.010$ result
quantifies the gap between these two notions; any claim about ``hallucination
detection'' in this work is shorthand for ``surface-form divergence detection.''}
\end{quote}

\subsection{Fisher Separability Computation}
\label{sec:setup-fisher}

Direct inversion of $\Sigma_w \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is numerically
unstable at $d = 2048$.  We apply two complementary approximations:
(i) \textbf{PCA projection} to $k = 100$ principal components
(capturing $> 95\%$ of variance in practice); and
(ii) \textbf{Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage}~\citep{ledoit2004} for covariance
regularization.  Both methods produce consistent $J$ estimates;
PCA projection is used throughout.  We note that for the Qwen 0.5B and
1.5B models at $n = 400$, the aspect ratio $\gamma = d/n > 1$
(values 2.24 and 3.84 respectively), making full-rank covariance estimation
unreliable; these results should be interpreted accordingly.

\section{The Geometric Certificate}
\label{sec:certificate}

\subsection{Derivation}
\label{sec:cert-derivation}

\begin{proposition}[Gaussian AUROC Certificate]
\label{prop:cert}
Let $P_c = \mathcal{N}(\mu_c, \Sigma_w)$ and
$P_h = \mathcal{N}(\mu_h, \Sigma_w)$ with equal covariance $\Sigma_w$.
The AUROC of the Bayes-optimal linear classifier equals
$\Phi\!\left(\nicefrac{\sqrt{J}}{2}\right)$
where $J = (\mu_c - \mu_h)^\top \Sigma_w^{-1} (\mu_c - \mu_h)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The optimal score is $s(h) = w^\top h$ with
$w = \Sigma_w^{-1}(\mu_c - \mu_h)$.
Under $P_c$: $s \sim \mathcal{N}(J,\, J)$;
under $P_h$: $s \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, J)$.
The standardised gap is $\sqrt{J}$, and
$\text{AUROC} = P(s_c > s_h) = \Phi\!\left(\nicefrac{\sqrt{J}}{2}\right)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Proposition~\ref{prop:cert} assumes Gaussian equal-covariance distributions.
Real hidden states violate both assumptions; Eq.~\eqref{eq:auroc-bound}
therefore serves as an approximation whose tightness we measure empirically.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Layer Selection Problem}
\label{sec:cert-layer}

Fisher $J$ cannot identify the optimal probing layer.  In all five CV folds,
argmax$_L\, J(L)$ selects L25--L26, while oracle best probe layers range from
L10 to L36 across folds.  A depth-weighted heuristic
($J(L) \times L / L_{\max}$) achieves 20\% match rate---better than
argmax but still poor.

\begin{remark}
The failure arises because $J$ measures second-order geometry (class
separation by covariance-weighted distance), whereas probe AUROC reflects
the total discriminative structure including local density variations and
higher-order moments.  High $J$ implies high \emph{achievable} AUROC; it
does not identify which layer's probe will realise that potential.
\end{remark}

This is a fundamental limitation: separability does not imply accessibility
by simple probes.  The practical implication: the certificate is useful for
deciding \emph{whether} to probe, not \emph{where}.

\caption{Fisher bound $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ (solid), linear probe AUROC
  (dashed), and MLP probe AUROC (dotted) at every layer.  The shaded
  region is the bound slack.  The bound is never violated by the MLP probe
  on either model.}
\label{fig:mlp}
\end{figure}

Four findings emerge.
\textit{First}, the MLP probe outperforms the linear probe at $21/37$
layers (Qwen) and $10/25$ layers (GPT-2), confirming that some nonlinear
structure exists in the representations.  The margin is small:
$+0.09\%$ and $+0.07\%$ at best layer respectively.
\textit{Second}, the Fisher bound $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ holds across all
$37 + 25 = 62$ layer--model combinations (zero violations).  The Gaussian
slack is sufficient to absorb the additional nonlinear capacity.
\textit{Third}, the mean bound gap narrows only marginally for MLP
vs.\ linear ($0.0133 \to 0.0129$ on Qwen; essentially unchanged on GPT-2).
The MLP does not approach the Gaussian ceiling any more closely than the
linear probe does.
\textit{Fourth}, the MLP and linear probes identify different optimal
layers (Qwen: L20 vs.\ L22; GPT-2: L19 vs.\ L8), compounding the
layer-selection failure of Section~\ref{sec:cert-layer}.

\begin{remark}
The bound holds empirically but not theoretically: the Gaussian derivation
is specific to linear classifiers.  A tighter nonlinear certificate---one
that accounts for higher-order moments---remains an open problem.
What this result \emph{does} establish is that, for the shallow nonlinear
probes tested here, the Gaussian slack is sufficient: the approximation
$\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ functions as an empirical upper bound for both linear
and nonlinear probes on these representations.
\end{remark}

\section{Beyond Gaussianity: Wasserstein Certificates}
\label{sec:ot}

\subsection{The Fisher--Wasserstein Identity and Its Violation}
\label{sec:ot-identity}

Under Gaussian equal-covariance assumptions, Fisher separability and
Wasserstein-2 distance in whitened space are identical:
$J = W_2^2\!\left(\tilde{P}_c, \tilde{P}_h\right)$
where $\tilde{P} = \Sigma_w^{-1/2} P$.
We verify this identity at five representative layers per model (Exp.~08).
Table~\ref{tab:identity} reports relative errors
$\epsilon = |J - W_{2,\text{wh}}^2| / J$.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Relative error of $J \approx W_2^2$ (whitened) identity at
  five layers per model (Exp.~08).  Identity never holds; error grows
  with depth for Qwen.}
\label{tab:identity}
\small
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
\begin{tabular}{lcccccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{L0} & \textbf{L$\nicefrac{1}{4}$} &
  \textbf{L$\nicefrac{1}{2}$} & \textbf{L$\nicefrac{3}{4}$} &
  \textbf{L$_{\max}$} & \textbf{Mean} \\
\midrule
Qwen 2.5 3B  & 0.72 & 1.43 & 1.64 & 1.76 & 3.01 & 1.71 \\
GPT-2 Medium & 0.54 & 0.83 & 0.87 & 0.75 & 0.88 & 0.77 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The identity $J = W_2^2$ fails across all layers: mean relative error
$1.71$ for Qwen and $0.77$ for GPT-2.  This is a \emph{finding}---hidden-state
distributions are measurably non-Gaussian---not a flaw in the experimental
design.

A plausible mechanistic explanation: in Qwen~2.5~3B, later layers compress

divergence (SW$_2$) a better proxy for linear separability.  In GPT-2~Medium,
the probe peak at $33\%$ depth (L8) precedes the representational compression
phase, so distributional divergence and probe performance evolve in opposite
directions.
\end{remark}

\section{Conformal Coverage Guarantees}
\label{sec:conformal}

\subsection{Calibration and Results}
\label{sec:conformal-results}

At $\alpha^* = 0.07$, the system must reject $47.1\%$ of queries to
achieve the coverage guarantee.  This acceptance-rate cost is the honest

\subsection{Distribution Shift: Guarantee Collapse}
\label{sec:conformal-ood}

We test the calibrated threshold from HaluEval on 400 TruthfulQA
questions---a genuinely different task distribution.  Results:
AUROC $= 0.485$ (chance level); $98.2\%$ of TruthfulQA responses are
hallucinated under ROUGE-L labels; acceptance rate $= 99.95\%$ (nearly
all accepted).  The conformal guarantee, calibrated at $\alpha^* = 0.07$
on HaluEval, would report an empirical hallucination rate of $0.982$
on TruthfulQA---a $14\times$ violation.  This collapse is consistent with
\citet{dubanowska2025ood}, who show that representation-based hallucination
detectors broadly fail to generalise out of distribution, even when
in-distribution AUROC exceeds $0.98$.

\begin{remark}
The guarantee holds only under exchangeability (i.i.d.\ with the
calibration distribution).  OOD shift violates this assumption.
Distribution-robust conformal prediction~\citep{tibshirani2019conformal}
is the principled extension, but requires density ratio estimation
between calibration and deployment distributions.
\end{remark}

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]

\item \textbf{Label quality.}  Cohen's $\kappa = -0.010$ between ROUGE-L
  and GPT-4o labels (Exp.~07) means every AUROC and every bound in this
  paper conditions on a labelling criterion that a semantic judge largely
  disagrees with.  Future work should establish a validated labelling
  protocol---ideally with inter-annotator agreement on factual
  incorrectness---before drawing strong empirical conclusions.

\item \textbf{Gaussian violations and bound looseness.}  The Gaussian
  equal-covariance assumption is violated ($J \neq W_2^2$, mean relative
  error up to $1.71$).  Bound errors of $1$--$3\%$ result.  Fisher $J$
  uniformly over-predicts AUROC, but only by amounts small enough to be
  practically useful.

\item \textbf{Layer selection.}  Argmax $J$ never identifies the best
  probe layer (0\% match over 5 CV folds).  The certificate bounds
  achievable performance; it does not locate the layer that achieves it.

\item \textbf{Scale curve is interpolation.}  $R^2 = 0.9996$ on three
  data points with two parameters is trivially high.  We interpret the
  result as demonstrating monotonic growth, not as a scaling law.

\item \textbf{Aspect ratio violation at small scale.}  Qwen 0.5B
  ($\gamma = 2.24$) and 1.5B ($\gamma = 3.84$) have $d > n$ at $n = 400$.
  Covariance estimation is unreliable in this regime; Ledoit--Wolf
  shrinkage mitigates but does not resolve this.

\item \textbf{Architecture transfer unresolved.}  We attempted transfer
  to Mamba-130m (SSM, 25 layers) but the experiment was confounded by the
  absence of instruction tuning: all responses were labelled hallucinated
  (hall rate $= 1.0$), making classification meaningless.  SSM
  architecture transfer is an open question.

\item \textbf{Conformal guarantee is distribution-conditional.}  The
  $\alpha^* = 0.07$ guarantee holds only on HaluEval.  OOD applications
  require covariate-shift conformal methods.

\item \textbf{SW$_2$ advantage is model-dependent.}  SW$_2$ outperforms
  Fisher on Qwen but underperforms on GPT-2.  No universally superior
  distribution-free certificate exists across the models tested.

\item \textbf{Nonlinear probe bound is empirical, not theoretical.}
  Section~\ref{sec:mlp-probe} evaluates a shallow MLP probe (64 hidden,
  ReLU, Adam) against the Fisher certificate.  The bound holds empirically
  across all 62 layer--model pairs (zero violations); MLP gain over linear
  is $\leq 0.09\%$.  However, the Gaussian derivation applies strictly to
  linear classifiers.  A principled nonlinear certificate, potentially
  tighter than $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$, remains an open theoretical question.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Representation probing for hallucination.}
\citet{burns2022discovering} show that LLM hidden states encode linearly
separable truthfulness signals.  \citet{azaria2023internal} (SAPLMA) apply
this to GPT-2, establishing a global linear probe baseline.
\citet{chen2024inside} (INSIDE) use global covariance eigenvalues;
\citet{kossen2024semantic} train probes to predict semantic entropy
from single-pass hidden states with OOD robustness.
\citet{du2024haloscope} project embeddings onto an SVD hallucination
subspace.  Our work differs by providing a \emph{training-free} bound
on what any such probe can achieve, rather than training a probe itself.

\paragraph{Geometric and distributional structure.}
\citet{marks2024geometry} show that truth representations exhibit emergent
linear structure across LLM families.  \citet{belrose2023eliciting} track
prediction formation layer-by-layer via the tuned lens.
\citet{bao2025probing} probe truth directions across logical transformations,
demonstrating that linear truth geometry generalises across question reformulations---
complementing our finding that Fisher $J$ provides a stable upper bound across
layers.  None of these works derive a computable, closed-form certificate from
the geometry.

\paragraph{Concurrent geometric classifiers.}
\citet{ricco2026geometric} apply Fisher separability as a classification
boundary in small-model embedding space, achieving F1~$>90\%$ on hallucination
detection.  This is the closest concurrent work.  The distinction is precise:
\citeauthor{ricco2026geometric} exploit the geometry operatively (as a
classifier); this paper characterises the geometry diagnostically (as a
certificate bounding what any classifier can achieve).  The two contributions
address different questions and are complementary: the certificate tells you
\emph{whether} to build a Fisher classifier; the classifier tells you \emph{how}.

\paragraph{OOD failure of representation-based detectors.}
\citet{dubanowska2025ood} (EMNLP~2025) show that representation-based
hallucination detectors fail to generalise out-of-distribution across
benchmarks, even when in-distribution performance is high.  This provides
independent empirical motivation for the OOD collapse documented in
Section~\ref{sec:conformal-ood}: AUROC $\to 0.485$ and a $14\times$
conformal violation when the HaluEval-calibrated threshold is applied to
TruthfulQA.  The certificate developed here makes the failure predictable:
a geometry trained on one distribution provides no coverage guarantee on
another.

\paragraph{Conformal prediction.}
\citet{angelopoulos2022} provide the foundational framework.
\citet{tibshirani2019conformal} extend conformal prediction to handle
distribution shift via importance weighting---the principled extension
needed for OOD deployment of the certificate derived here.
\citet{abbasi2024conformal} apply conformal abstention to LLM generation,
requiring multiple samples; our method uses a single forward pass.

\paragraph{Output-level detection.}
\citet{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} (SelfCheckGPT) detect hallucination via
multi-sample output consistency without accessing hidden states.
\citet{li2023halueval} provide the HaluEval benchmark.

\paragraph{Local geometry and KNN methods.}
\citet{sun2022ood} use KNN distance (not class separability) for OOD
detection in image models.  \citet{sugiyama2007} establish local Fisher
discriminant analysis at training time.  Our approach uses Fisher
separability in KNN neighbourhoods at inference time (the GUARDIAN system,
described separately), addressing the global/local failure documented
in multi-step pipelines.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Fisher separability constitutes a computable, training-free geometric
certificate for linear probe performance in hallucination detection.
The approximation $\Phi(\sqrt{J}/2)$ matches probe AUROC within $2.7\%$
maximum error across all tested layers and architectures, and anticipates
held-out fold AUROC within $0.93\%$ mean error in cross-validation---before
a single label is used for training.  A split conformal guarantee
($\alpha^* = 0.07$) then converts this certificate into a formal
in-distribution bound on hallucination rate among accepted queries.

The failure modes are equally informative, and each is precisely scoped:
the certificate cannot identify the optimal probing layer (0\% argmax
match across five folds); the Gaussian assumption is violated ($J \neq W_2^2$,
mean error $1.71$ for Qwen); the SW$_2$ advantage over Fisher is
architecture-dependent (reversed on GPT-2~Medium); and the conformal
guarantee collapses under distribution shift (AUROC $\to 0.485$, TruthfulQA).

These failures are not incidental: they constitute a roadmap.
Three directions are immediately actionable.
First, distribution-free certificates via Wasserstein geometry can replace
the Gaussian assumption where it is most violated (deep layers of Qwen).
Second, local geometry adaptation via KNN neighbourhoods addresses the OOD
collapse, extending the certificate to deployment-distribution mixtures---as
documented in the companion GUARDIAN system~\citep{sun2022ood}.
Third, validated labelling protocols beyond ROUGE-L are prerequisite for
extending the certificate to factual hallucination rather than surface-form
divergence; the $\kappa = -0.010$ result quantifies exactly how large that gap
currently is.

More broadly, the central finding reframes a methodological question.
Hallucination detection has largely been pursued as a supervised learning
problem: collect labels, train a probe, measure AUROC.  The certificate
establishes that AUROC is predictable from geometry \emph{before} that
pipeline begins---meaning representation quality, not probe sophistication,
is the binding constraint.  This shifts the design question from
\emph{which classifier to train} to \emph{which representations to build}.
